Circuit Design (Project 1.2.6)
October 2024

Our goal for the P1.2.6 Circuit Design project was to create a traffic control system that could handle a small-scale intersection in real time. The idea was to use basic combinational and sequential logic to dynamically manage car traffic and pedestrian crossings based on their presence. The system would simulate real-world functionality using logic gates, LEDs, and flip-flops to ensure traffic flowed efficiently and safely.

At the start, I was very interested in creating flip-flops using NAND gates to store information about active traffic and pedestrian signals. I thought it would be a cool way to build our own variables from scratch and make the project more unique; it quickly became clear that this approach was way too ambitious for the tools we had available. Trying to cram that level of complexity onto a breadboard would take far more time and resources than we had available to us, so I had to dial it back focus on using direct user input rather than variables, which let us focus on getting the rest of the circuit working.

Despite careful planning in the simulation part of the project (using Multisim), the project encountered significant challenges. During the presentation, the timing portion of the circuit failed to function due to a malfunctioning AND gate, which disrupted our ability to showcase its functionality in its entirety. After the presentation, the issue was resolved by replacing the faulty component, at which point the system performed as designed. Reflecting on this experience, we should have finished earlier to do rigorous physical testing and redundancy checks to prevent similar issues in the future. The presentation itself could also have benefited from better preparation in terms of organizing the flow and clearly assigning speaking roles.

The design incorporated various features to manage the intersection effectively. Combinational logic, utilizing AND gates, OR gates, and inverters, ensured that vehicles could only proceed in one direction at a time. Similarly, crosswalk lights were activated only when no vehicles were detected on the corresponding street. The sequential logic component included a 15-second pedestrian crossing timer implemented with a divide-by-two circuit and flip-flops, making sure pedestrians have enough time to cross the intersection. This integration of sequential and combinational logic required careful consideration of the system's physical implementation, especially in differences between timers in the simulation and on the breadboard.

In the project, my specific responsibilities were outlining the logic for the program in the documentation (linked below), handling the combinational logic component of the simulation/design, and building the project on a breadboard. Despite the setbacks, this project taught me a lot about being realistic with design goals and adapting when things don’t go as planned. While my original vision of constructing flip-flops from NAND gates was not feasible, adapting to the constraints instead of spending all of our time on one part allowed us to deliver a working prototype. It also reminded me that good organization and preparation are just as important as the technical side of a project—-even more so when working on a team.

Overall, this project was a valuable learning experience that taught me the importance of adapting to challenges and balancing my ambition with practicality. Despite some setbacks, like the faulty AND gate during the presentation, seeing our traffic control system work helped my understanding of circuit design and problem-solving. It was rewarding to turn an idea into a functional system and explore how what I've been learning can be, and has been, applied to real-world challenges like traffic control.
